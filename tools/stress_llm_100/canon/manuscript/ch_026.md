# Chapter 26: Echoes in the Crimson

The light in the glacial chamber was dying. Not the steady, ancient glow of the ice-veined walls, but a more vital, more human light. Kael felt it ebbing within him as he leaned against a frozen buttress, each breath a shallow struggle. His skin was pallid, stretched too thinly over bones that seemed to ache with a newfound brittleness. When he moved his hand, a faint, spectral afterimage trailed a half-second behind it—a visible lag in his own timeline.

Lira worked with a frantic, focused energy a few feet away. Her satchel was spilled across the ice, scrolls and artifacts splayed out. She had taken a sliver of the Obsidian Heart’s shadow, a mere flake of darkness chipped from the cavern wall where its influence pooled, and was studying it under a crystal lens. Her ears, usually so attuned, twitched not at sounds, but at silences, as if listening for the sound of seconds being erased.

“The texts from the Sunstrider archives,” she murmured, not looking up. “They spoke of the ‘Time-Scarred,’ warriors who brushed against temporal rifts. They didn’t wither away, Kael. They… frayed. Events in their past became mutable, their futures indistinct.” She finally lifted her gaze, her silver eyes filled with a scholar’s horror and a friend’s anguish. “Your blood on the ice. It’s not just an injury. It’s a scar on the chronology. A point where your personal timeline is… thin.”

Kael tried to muster a warrior’s scoff, but it died in a cough. “A weakness.” The word was bitter. Dawnbreaker felt like a lead weight at his hip, its dawn-light now seeming faint, hesitant, as if mirroring its wielder’s fading vitality. The vision—the burning sun, Lira’s fall—was no longer just a warning; it felt like a debt coming due. He had touched the Heart, and now it was collecting its first installment.

“Not just a weakness,” Lira corrected, her voice dropping to a whisper. She pointed at the obsidian flake under her lens. “Look.”

He forced himself forward, his steps lagging. Where the flake lay, the eternal blue ice beneath it was changing. The flawless crystal was developing fine, hairline fractures, not from pressure, but from age. In the span of a dozen heartbeats, the tiny area beneath the flake aged a century, the ice becoming cloudy and brittle before crumbling into fine, dry powder.
