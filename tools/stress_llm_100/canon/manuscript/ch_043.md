# Chapter 43: The Price of Rewind

The frost on Kael’s skin was no longer cold. It *burned*. Each crystalline pattern pulsed with a heat that seared inward, bypassing flesh to scorch something deeper—the very stream of his consciousness. He saw not one Lira kneeling before him, but a flickering, layered specter. In one moment, her eyes were wide with concern; in the next, they were clouded with the dust of ages; in a third, they were young, bright, studying a text in a sunlit Thalassian library.

“Your eyes…” Lira whispered, her hand finally touching his forehead. She recoiled, not from heat, but from a sense of temporal vertigo, as if she’d touched a spinning gyroscope.

“It’s showing me… everything,” Kael gasped. The glacier around him seemed to thin, its substance becoming translucent. Through it, he saw not rock, but images. A sun setting over Valdris in a single, stuttering heartbeat. A tree growing from sapling to ancient giant and crumbling to dust in the time it took his heart to beat once. “The Heart. It’s not just showing me time. It’s… *unstitching* me.”

The vision from the black plain—the one he’d tried to call a dream—crystallized with brutal clarity. He stood before the Obsidian Heart, a pulsing shard of midnight, not in some hallowed temple, but in a ruined city that looked like a twisted version of Eryndor. And he understood its mechanism with the certainty of a primal instinct.

It didn’t move you through time. It *spent* you. To bend an hour, it would drink a year of your life. To reverse a day, it would consume a decade. To reclaim the dead… the cost was incalculable. A soul for a moment. A lifetime for a single, borrowed dawn.

And the frost was the first withdrawal.

“It’s a debt, Lira,” he said, his voice hoarse with this terrible knowledge. “Every shift, every echo of what was or what could be… the Heart takes its payment. In vitality. In years. In *self*.”

Lira’s scholarly fascination warred with dawning horror. “What have you already paid, Kael?”
