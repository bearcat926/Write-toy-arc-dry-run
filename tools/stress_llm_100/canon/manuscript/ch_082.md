# Chapter 82: The Wound in Eternity

Dawnbreaker struck not flesh, but the seam where obsidian met the mortal remnant of Marcus’s shoulder. The impact was silent, yet a shockwave of pure, timeless light erupted, momentarily freezing the decaying vortex in place. Kael felt the vibration travel up his arm, a harmonic dissonance against the Heart’s song.

For a heartbeat, Marcus was just a man again, his eyes wide with shock, the obsidian growth still sleek and whole. Then the wound began. Not bleeding, but *fraying*. Threads of darkness, like pulled silk, unwound from the impact point, dissolving into the air. The edges of the obsidian crust around the gash began to calcify and crumble, shedding dust that had not been allowed to age in millennia.

A sound escaped Marcus, a gasp that held millennia of stolen time. His good hand clawed at the crumbling edge of his wound, trying to hold it together. “You… cannot… unmake what I have become,” he rasped, his voice a chorus of echoes.

“The Heart is unmade,” Lira breathed from the pillar’s shadow, her scholar’s mind grappling with the visible proof. “Dawnbreaker doesn’t fight the time… it restores the *moment*. It makes the corruption obey the law of *now*.”

The frozen decay around them shuddered, then lurched back into hideous motion, but it was different. Chaotic. Unfocused. The Sanctum, once a controlled nightmare, was now reacting to its master’s injury. Marcus stumbled, one knee hitting the cracked marble. The temporal field around him warped, causing him to flicker—aged a hundred years, then regressed to a child, then back—before stabilizing into his wounded, human form.

Kael pressed forward, raising Dawnbreaker for a second strike. The golden light pulsed eagerly, but a deep exhaustion settled into his bones. Holding the blade against this entropy was like holding back an ocean with his bare hands.

Marcus looked up, and in his fractured eyes, Kael saw not just the commander’s hatred, but a terrifying, desperate hunger. The hunger of a god who has tasted mortality’s flaw. “The cost is paid,” Marcus whispered, and the obsidian heart in his chest pulsed once, violently. The wound in his shoulder stopped fraying. The crumbling edges sealed over, not with new flesh, but with a smoother, darker obsidian, a permanent scar upon the corruption.

He was healing, but not returning to what he was. He was evolving.
