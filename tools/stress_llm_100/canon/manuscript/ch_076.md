# Chapter 76: The Price of All Tomorrows

The lattice of light and script began to fray. A single, fraying thread of golden light from Dawnbreaker snapped, dissolving into motes that fell upward, defying the Sanctum’s warped physics. Kael felt the snap in his bones, a hollow ache that spoke of more than fatigue. It was a piece of his connection to the blade, and to the present, being unmade.

“Hold, Kael!” Lira’s voice was thin, strained. She knelt before the pulsing Heart, her hands pressed flat against the cool, dark stone. Her eyes were closed, and the elven script that flowed from her fingertips glowed not with her own life, but with the borrowed light of her people’s oldest histories. Each character she inscribed was a memory torn from the Great Archives of her mind, offered as a bulwark against the temporal decay.

But the Heart was hungry. It devoured her anchors not with malice, but with the impersonal greed of a starving void. A scene flickered into existence around them—the Sanctum whole, but filled with robed figures. One, a woman with Lira’s cheekbones and the same silver-white hair, turned and offered a look of profound pity. The image was sucked into the Heart’s dark core, and Lira gasped as if struck.

“You’re burning them all,” Kael rasped, his own vision tunneling. He saw Dawnbreaker not as a gleaming sword, but as a rusted shard embedded in the skeletal remains of his ancestor. The future memory was a brutal gut-punch.

“It is the only currency the Obsidian accepts!” Lira cried, her voice cracking. “A life’s memory for a moment’s purchase. I must give it everything, Kael! All the futures I might have had. All the knowledge I might yet discover. It is… the anchor it needs.”

The light of her script flared, blindingly white, and Kael saw not just her, but a thousand Miras—a thousand possible histories of her lineage—converge into that single, sacrificial offering. The Sanctum’s shuddering ceased. The temporal ghosts stilled, trapped in a fragile stasis within the lattice. But at the center, the Obsidian Heart began to spin, slowly, its darkness coalescing into a single, tangible point.

It was a tear. A perfect, vertical rip in the air, through which no light passed.

“Lira, stop!” Kael roared, taking a step forward. The ground beneath him solidified, no longer rippling. The cost of her memory was buying them a dangerous stability.
