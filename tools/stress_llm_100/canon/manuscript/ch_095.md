# Chapter 95: The Shattered Oath

Kael stood, the motion slow and deliberate, as if each muscle remembered a different history than the one he’d lived. The golden light of Dawnbreaker no longer felt like a blessing from the Sun; it felt like a leash.

“You knew,” he said, his voice hollow, directed at Marcus but also at the twisting blood-memory inside him. “You knew what I was.”

“I knew what you *are*,” Marcus corrected, circling the Heart with a proprietor’s ease. “The last trueborn of the Oath-Binders. The key to controlling this. Your clan didn’t just guard the Heart, Kael. They were its sheath. Its wardens. And your ancestors were cowards who buried it rather than fulfill its purpose.”

From the edge of the dais, Lira’s sharp intake of breath was audible. “The Oath-Binders… they are legend. A line said to have made a covenant with time itself.”

“A covenant they broke,” Marcus spat, his calm finally fracturing. “Leaving the Heart to fracture. To bleed. To create… anomalies. Like the one that turned Valdris to salt and sand.” He gestured, and in the air before them, reality shimmered. Not a memory this time, but a *window*. They saw a sun-drenched city, streets bustling with life, its banners bearing the sunburst of Valdris. Then, a wave of dissonant energy, a sound like breaking glass played backward, and the city *flickered*. For a second, it was replaced by a barren, white-salt desert, before snapping back to its living form. “That is a tear. A consequence of the Heart’s agony. Left unchecked, it will unravel all that is.”

Kael’s grip on Dawnbreaker tightened, not to strike, but to anchor himself. The weapon hummed, a low, confused vibration. It could not banish this threat; this threat was woven into the fabric of his own blood. “What is the purpose you speak of?”

“To restore the covenant! To use the Heart, through an Oath-Binder’s blood and will, to heal the rifts. To stabilize the timeline.” Marcus’s eyes burned with a fanatic’s light. “But it requires sacrifice. A willing binding. The Heart consumes to mend. It needs a willing life to fuel the correction.”

The cold truth settled in Kael’s gut. The terrible cost was not just life, but *a* life. A specific, willing life. His life.
