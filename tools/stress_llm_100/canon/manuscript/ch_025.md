# Chapter 25: The Lingering Scar

The blood on the ice was not the worst of it. As Kael allowed Lira to pull him to his feet, he felt a new fragility in his bones, a thinning of the very substance of him. The seconds the Heart had stolen were not gone; they were a hollow space inside his timeline, a wound that whispered.

"The vision," he rasped, his voice rough. "Valdris. It burns. You... you were falling."

Lira’s grip on his arm tightened. Her scholarly calm was gone, replaced by a sharp, protective intensity. "Temporal feedback," she said, her eyes scanning the hovering obsidian. "The Heart doesn't just show you a future, Kael. It pulls a sliver of that future's *weight* back through you. What you saw was not a dream. It was an echo of an event that, to this artifact, has already passed. Or is passing."

He looked at the droplets of his life, stark against the eternal blue. "It cost me... how much time?"

"Does it matter?" she countered, her tone fierce. "The cost is always enough. The chronicles were clear. The Heart grants sight, but it mortgages the seer's lifespan to do so. You are now more entangled with that burning future than ever."

A new sound reached them—not the glacial heartbeat, but the scrape of armored boots on stone from the cave entrance. Lira’s eyes widened. "We are out of time in another sense entirely."

Marcus stepped into the luminescence, his Shadow Council armor drinking the light. He was not alone; two figures flanked him, cloaked in the same shifting darkness. His eyes fell first on the Obsidian Heart, and a slow, covetous smile touched his lips. Then his gaze landed on Kael, on the blood, on his stance of a man bearing an invisible weight.

"The Sunstrider bleeds for his prize," Marcus said, his voice echoing in the chamber. "Good. The passage of a living soul makes the key... more compliant." He raised a gauntleted hand, not toward Kael, but toward the Heart. The dark veins within it pulsed in answer. "You have shown me the lock. Now, step aside."

Kael’s hand went to Dawnbreaker’s hilt. The sword hummed, its golden light flaring against the encroaching shadows. The memory of Valdris in flames, of Lira’s fall, crystallized not into despair, but into a cold, furious clarity. He was not fighting for a kingdom or a prophecy now. He was fighting to unwrite a sentence he had read in his own blood.
