# Chapter 24: The Weight of Seconds

The coppery taste in Kael’s mouth was his own blood. He stared at the droplets staining the blue ice, stark and crimson, a tiny, mortal counterpoint to the vast, timeless power hovering in the chamber. The vision of Valdris burning, of Lira falling, was seared not just onto his mind, but into his bones.

“The cost,” Lira breathed, her voice a fragile whisper in the frozen silence. She didn’t move toward him, her eyes fixed on the Heart. “The chronal bleed. It’s not just a toll on the world, Kael. It’s on the wielder. Your life-thread… it’s fraying.”

Kael wiped his nose with the back of his gauntlet, a rough, angry gesture. “Good. It shows the vision was real.” He pushed himself to his feet, his joints protesting the unnatural cold. “I saw it, Lira. Everything. The sky shattered, the sun a dead thing in the heavens. And you…” His voice broke.

She finally looked at him, her scholar’s composure fracturing. “A possible future. Not a certain one. The Heart shows potentials, drawn from the flow of time it perceives. It is a map, not a destination.”

“It felt like a destination.” He gestured at the obsidian sphere. “It showed me a way to stop it. A thread to pull. But the vision ended, cut off by… pain.”

Lira’s gaze sharpened, her mind wrestling the horror into the familiar shape of a problem. “A limit. Or a safeguard. The artifact won’t—or can’t—show the full consequences of altering an event it itself sets in motion. A closed loop of causality.” She took a cautious step closer, not to the Heart, but to him. “What thread, Kael? What did it show you to do?”

“Not a thing,” he said, the realization cold and heavy. “A place. And a time. The Royal Archives of Eryndor. Before the Scholar’s Purge. A specific scroll, stored in the deep vaults.”

“A temporal anchor,” Lira whispered, her breath misting. “To retrieve knowledge lost for centuries. Knowledge that could arm Valdris, or expose the Shadow Council’s true origins. It’s brilliant, and suicidal. The temporal displacement required would be immense. The cost…”
