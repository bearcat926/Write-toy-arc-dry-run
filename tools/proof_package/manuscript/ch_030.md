# Chapter 30: The Heart's Bargain

Marcus’s shadow stretched before him, clawing across the ice toward the pulsating Obsidian Heart. “You’ve paid dearly for your peek behind the curtain, Sunstrider. Now, step aside. The Shadow Council will claim what Valdris was too foolish to wield.”

Kael tried to push himself up, but his arms trembled. Dawnbreaker, lying beside him, felt impossibly heavy. He had spent what he did not have to save Lira. Now, there was nothing left.

But Lira did not move. She stood between Kael and the artifact, her scholar’s robes billowing in a phantom wind that had sprung from the Heart’s deepening thrum. Her eyes, usually calm and calculating, burned with a desperate, fierce intelligence.

“The price is not just time, Commander,” she said, her voice cutting through the chamber’s groan. “It’s memory. It’s self. It unravels the tapestry from the inside.”

Marcus laughed, a dry, hollow sound. “A fairytale to frighten children. Power always has a cost. We are willing to pay.” He took another step, his hand outstretched.

Lira’s gaze flicked from Marcus to Kael’s pale face, to the Heart. A decision crystallized in her eyes, swift and terrible. “Then let the first thread be yours.”

Before Kael could cry out, she lunged. Not for Marcus, but for the Heart. Her slender fingers closed around the jagged black stone.

The world inverted.

Light became sound, a high, keening note that vibrated in the marrow. Time stuttered, not backward or forward, but sideways. Kael saw Lira as she was and as she might be—a thousand possibilities shimmering around her form. He saw Marcus’s advance freeze, his cruel smile suspended.

Lira screamed, but no sound emerged. Instead, the Heart pulsed once, violently, and a wave of absolute shadow erupted from it, not to consume, but to *erase*. It struck Marcus not as a force, but as an absence. For a split-second, Kael saw through him—saw the ice beyond, the motes of dust in the air where his torso should be. The commander’s eyes widened in pure, naked terror before the wave passed. He collapsed like a puppet with its strings cut, unconscious but alive, the obsidian sigil on his breastplate now dull and cracked.

The chamber fell silent. The Heart’s glow receded to a sullen ember in Lira’s palm.

She turned to Kael, her face a mask of shock. Her elven eyes, once a vivid emerald, were now a muted, cloudy grey. She swayed, catching herself on a jut of ice.

“Lira!” Kael found the strength to crawl to her.
