# Chapter 40: The Temporal Frost

The frost was no longer just on the canvas. It bloomed across Kael’s forearms in intricate, crystalline patterns, each one a tiny, perfect map of somewhere he had never been. Lira watched, her scholar’s mind cataloging the horror even as her heart fractured. He didn’t shiver anymore. The cold was him.

“Kael,” she whispered, her breath a white plume in the glacial air of their refuge. “Talk to me. What do you see?”

His eyes, once the color of a summer sky, were now a pale, frost-shocked grey. They focused slowly on her. “The glacier’s fall. Not as it happened. As it *will* happen again. And again. The echoes are… stacking.” His voice was a dry rustle, like wind through dead leaves. “The Heart didn’t just vanish. It pushed us all… out of sync.”

Lira’s fingers hovered over the satchel at her hip. The star-chart was useless here, its maps of fixed heavens mocking a world untethered from time. But inside, wrapped in oiled silk, was another prize from the Eryndor royal library—a lens of smoked quartz, said to reveal temporal distortions. A desperate, long-shot tool. She drew it out, its surface unnaturally cool.

Through the lens, the world leached of color. Kael became a figure of static, a human silhouette caught in a blizzard of shimmering, displaced moments—his form flickering between standing, kneeling, and something that looked terrifyingly like falling backward into nothingness. But it was the frost on his arms that showed the truth. Through the lens, the crystalline patterns were not white. They pulsed with a deep, inner blackness, like veins of night sky under his skin. They were anchoring him, pulling fragments of his own timeline into the present and freezing them there.

“It’s not a sickness,” she breathed, lowering the lens. “It’s a tether. The frost… it’s using your own moments to stabilize you in this fractured state. It’s saving you by burying you in yourself.”

A ghost of his old, wry smile touched his lips. “A fitting tomb.” He pushed himself up, the movement unnervingly fluid for a man supposedly freezing from the inside out. The frost patterns on his arms briefly flared with dark light. “We can’t stay. Marcus will have this region scoured.”

He was right. The distant horn blast had been a herald, not a singular event. They had perhaps an hour.
