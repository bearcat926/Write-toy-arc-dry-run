# Chapter 31: The Echoes of What Was

The Obsidian Heart’s pulse was a living thing, a drumbeat that resonated in the hollow spaces of Kael’s soul. His strength was gone, traded for breath, but his mind—sharp, clear, and utterly terrified—saw the path. Marcus’s shadow loomed, his hand outstretched.

“You are too late, commander,” Kael whispered, his voice a dry rasp. He didn’t move toward his fallen sword. Instead, he fixed his gaze on the artifact. “It has already shown me the cost. And the key.”

Lira’s gasp was soft. “Kael, no. You don’t understand what you’re saying. The memories it claims… they are not yours to bargain.”

But he did understand. The Heart had not healed him; it had rearranged him, trading a future of months for a past of minutes. And in that terrible, intimate exchange, it had whispered its own mechanics to his fractured spirit. It didn’t manipulate time; it devoured it, devouring the moments that made a person whole. Lira’s sacrifice of a hundred years of memory had given him a heartbeat. His own memory of his mother’s face was a flickering ember, already warming the Heart’s malevolent core.

Marcus laughed, a low, harsh sound. “The warrior tries philosophy. It’s over, Kael.”

“It is,” Kael agreed. He closed his eyes, not in defeat, but in focus. He did not reach for the Heart with his hands. He reached for it with the very essence of his borrowed time, with the silent, screaming void where his strength once lived. *Take this,* he thought, not in words but in a visceral, desperate pulse of will. *Take the echo of this moment. Take the memory of his victory.*

He poured the sensation of Marcus’s approaching triumph into the artifact’s hungry void. He sacrificed the feeling of the commander’s cold shadow falling over him, the sound of his arrogant breath, the sight of his outstretched fingers mere inches from the obsidian surface. He offered the future memory of this very second.

The effect was instantaneous and terrifying.

Marcus froze, his hand hovering. A flicker of confusion crossed his face. His eyes lost focus for a fraction of a second, and when they cleared, the confidence was gone, replaced by a primal, disoriented fear. He looked at his hand as if it were a foreign object, then at the Heart, his expression one of utter bewilderment. He couldn’t remember why he was reaching for it. The tactical certainty, the moment of triumph—had been carved out of his immediate past.
