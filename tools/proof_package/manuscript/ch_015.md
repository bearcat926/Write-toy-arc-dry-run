# Chapter 15: The Shadowed Flotilla

The wind at the Serpent’s Maw was a liar. It promised the open sea, freedom, and the distant shores of Nordmark, but Kael tasted only ash on his tongue. The memory-glass vision of Marcus, his eyes empty of malice and full of accusation, haunted him more than any shadow-wraith. *You did this. You chose the quick death over the hard question.*

“The ship is ready,” Lira said softly, her hand brushing his arm. Her touch was warm, a stark contrast to the chill settling in his bones. “A Thalassian cutter. Fast, if not sturdy. The captain owes a debt to the old alliances.”

Kael nodded, his gaze fixed on the frothing strait. The Sea of Ghosts, they called it, where currents ran like blood through a corpse’s veins. “The map’s path ends here. It speaks of a crossing under a ‘bleeding moon,’ but the moon is clear tonight.”

Lira unrolled the parchment copy, its faint, sorrowful whispers rising with the sea breeze. “Prophecy is rarely literal, Kael. The ‘bleeding’ may not be the moon’s, but our own. The Heart’s guardians demand sacrifice to even approach.” She pointed to a swirling glyph near Nordmark’s coast. “This symbol… it’s not just a location. It’s an echo of the Memory of Glass. It suggests the Heart’s chamber is a… mirror. A place where time’s wounds are reflected.”

Before Kael could respond, a cry rang from the cliffside watchman. Not of alarm, but of wonder, swiftly choked off by fear. Kael and Lira scrambled up the rocky path to the lookout’s post. The man pointed a trembling finger toward the eastern horizon.

There, where the grey sea met the grey sky, a flotilla emerged from the mist. Not the sturdy galleons of Thalassia, nor the dragon-prowed longships of Nordmark. These were sleek, black vessels, their sails bearing no insignia but a faint, pulsating sigil that hurt the eye—the interlocking circles of the Shadow Council. And at the lead, standing tall at the prow of the foremost ship, was a figure Kael knew.

He wore no armor, only a dark traveler’s cloak. But his bearing, the set of his shoulders, was unmistakable.

“Marcus,” Kael breathed, the word a shard of ice in his throat. It was impossible. He’d seen the life leave those eyes in the tomb.

“The Council,” Lira whispered, her scholar’s mind racing. “They didn’t just seek the Heart, they found a piece. They’ve used it. He’s not alive in the way we understand, Kael. He’s… *rewritten*. A echo given flesh and command.”

As if summoned by her words, the figure on the ship raised a hand.
