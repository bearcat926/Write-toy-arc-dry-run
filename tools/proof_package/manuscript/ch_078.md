# Chapter 78: The Obsidian’s Voice

The silence was a physical pressure against Kael’s eardrums. Marcus stood where the Obsidian Heart had pulsed, but it was no longer a separate artifact. It was fused to him, a jagged, crystalline growth erupting from his chest, its dark facets drinking the dim light of the Sanctum. Tendrils of solidified shadow, like frozen lightning, snaked from its core into Marcus’s armor, his skin, his very being.

“Kael, don’t—” Lira’s voice was a strained whisper.

But Kael had already shifted his weight, Dawnbreaker raised. The sword felt heavier, its inner light guttering as if starved. “What have you done, Marcus?” The commander’s face was a mask of agonized ecstasy, his eyes now twin voids that reflected no light.

“Done?” Marcus’s voice was a dissonant chorus, one voice his own, another a deep, resonant hum that seemed to vibrate the air itself. “I have become the answer. The anchor. You sought to cage time, scholar,” the void-eyes fixed on Lira, “to preserve. But preservation is stagnation. The Heart showed me.”

He took a step forward, and the stone floor beneath his boot did not crack; it aged, weathering centuries in an instant to dust. “It showed me every possible failure. Every timeline where Valdris falls, where Eryndor burns, where the Shadow Council is ground to dust by your petty heroics.” His gaze returned to Kael, filled not with hatred, but with a terrifying pity. “I have chosen the path that ensures *victory*. The Heart’s power is not to move through time, but to *remove* it from the equation. To make my decisions… absolute.”

Kael’s mind raced, the scholar’s explanations from long nights in libraries crashing against the warrior’s instinct. A terrible cost. The cost was Marcus himself. Or perhaps… everything else.

Lira’s hand found Kael’s arm, her touch icy. “He’s not controlling it. He’s becoming its vessel. The Obsidian Heart doesn’t manipulate time—it *consumes* it. It turns potential into power. And he’s feeding it every possibility but his own.”

“Clever,” the dual voice rumbled. “But too late.” Marcus raised a hand, and the air before him didn’t shimmer; it simply *ceased*. A perfect, rectangular hole of absolute nothingness appeared, cutting through a stone column, which remained standing only because the void had not yet reached its foundation. “This is my will now. The will of a single, unchangeable moment.”

He looked at Kael, and in the abyss of his eyes, Kael saw a flicker—a horrifying glimpse of a thousand Kaels, a thousand Dawnbreakers, all falling in a thousand different ways, all collapsing into this one, singular point of defeat.
