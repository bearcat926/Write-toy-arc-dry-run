# Chapter 60: The Whispering Bone

The shard was no bigger than a dagger’s pommel, and just as sharp. Kael pulled it from the flesh above his hip with a grunt, the pain a clean, bright counterpoint to the deep, nauseating throb of his crushed shoulder. In the near-total dark of the pocket, he could feel the wetness spreading on his tunic. Blood, not meltwater.

His hand, slick with it, brushed the hilt of Dawnbreaker lying beside him. The blade was cool. Inert. Its light was gone, smothered by the glacier’s corpse and the lingering, oily presence of the Obsidian Heart.

*You do not have to lie here and wait for the stone to become your tomb,* a voice whispered. It was not a sound, but a thought that did not feel entirely his own. It had a texture—like dry leaves skittering over slate.

Kael’s breath hitched. He tried to sit up, and the world swam. The whisper grew clearer, a thread of cold logic weaving through his pain.

*The bone is shattered. The muscle is torn. You will not walk from this place. Not as you are.*

“Lira,” he called out, his voice a ragged croak swallowed by the weight of the ruins above. Only the drip-drip-drip answered. She was gone. Snatched away in the final, violent shudder of the glacier, or buried elsewhere under a thousand tons of ice and rock.

*You could be whole. You could walk out. You could find her. You need only… choose. A small expenditure. A breath between ticks of a clock that has not yet run.*

The whisper wasn’t coming from the darkness. It was coming from the void where Dawnbreaker’s light should be. It was the echo of the Obsidian Heart, the memory of its touch, lodged in his own blood now. The cost. He understood, with a cold certainty, what it was asking for. Time. Not his own, perhaps, but time itself—a stolen moment from the past, a shaved second from the future. To knit his shoulder, to staunch his blood, would require a sacrifice from the weave of reality.

He lay still, listening to his pulse hammer in his ears. Each beat was a countdown. Each drip of water was a second passing that Lira might not have. His oath as a Sunstrider, his vow to his clan, to his companions—it warred with this intimate, selfish temptation offered by the void.

His fingers curled into the gritty, ice-dusted stone beneath him. He would not take from the river of time to save his own skin. He was Kael of the Sunstriders. He carried the Dawn.
