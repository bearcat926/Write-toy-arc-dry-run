# Chapter 13: The Memory of Glass

The weeping map was not a metaphor. Moisture, cold and slick as tears, beaded on the stone beneath Lira’s fingertips. As she pulled her hand back, a single drop clung to her skin, and in its surface, she saw not the dim tomb, but a cascade of impossible images: a village burning under a sky of three suns, a fleet of ships sailing on liquid silver, a man’s face contorted in silent scream.

She gasped, shaking the drop away. The visions vanished.

“The carvings… they’re not a map,” she whispered, her voice tight. “Not in the way we understand. They’re memory. The tomb is remembering.”

Kael stood, Dawnbreaker held loosely at his side. “Memory of what?”

“Of the Heart. Of those who tried to use it.” Lira’s eyes, usually bright with scholarly curiosity, were shadowed. “The prophecy calls it a key, Kael. But what I just saw… it was a price. The last cartographer didn’t just draw a path. He bled the memories of the fallen into the stone. It’s a ward, a warning made of lived despair.”

“Then the way forward is through those memories.” Kael’s tone was flat, resigned. He stepped to the wall, the faint light of his sword catching on the viscous sheen. He placed his palm flat against it. For a heartbeat, there was nothing. Then, the world dissolved.

He was not in a tomb. He stood in a sun-drenched courtyard, the architecture foreign, all sharp angles and blue-tiled domes. A woman with his mother’s eyes laughed as she chased a toddler—he recognized himself, hair a wild bronze halo. The peace was a physical blow.

Then the sky darkened. A crack opened in the air, a wound of obsidian light. From it stepped a figure in robes of woven shadow, their face a void. The laughing woman turned, her smile freezing. The child-Kael looked up, confused.

The shadow figure raised a hand. It did not attack the woman. It reached for the toddler.

Kael roared, but he was a ghost here. He could only watch as the figure’s fingers closed not around the child, but around a moment. The air rippled. The child vanished, reappearing ten feet away, unharmed but now wailing.
