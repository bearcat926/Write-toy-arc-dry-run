# Chapter 100: The Unmade Gate

The Memory Sea receded, leaving Kael kneeling on cold stone, Lira’s limp form cradled against his chest. The visions clung to him like salt-crusted scales—the fall of Eryndor’s Sky-Spires, the first pact sworn in blood, the centuries of guardianship that had calcified into dogma. Lira was not a traitor. She was a monument, worn down by time and duty.

He shifted her gently, propping her against a carved plinth. Dawnbreaker’s light pulsed softly, casting their shadows long and flickering against the chamber’s walls, which were etched with maps of continents that no longer existed. One map, larger than the rest, depicted a landmass without a name, centered on a crude, spiraling symbol: the Obsidian Heart.

“Wake up,” he murmured, brushing dust from her brow. “We are not done.”

Lira’s eyes fluttered open, not with her usual scholarly sharpness, but with the profound exhaustion of one who has recounted a thousand-year lament. “The cost… you saw it.”

“I saw a bargain,” Kael said, his voice rough. “You guarded the Heart. The Sunstriders were created to guard *you*. To be the weapon you could never be.” The words tasted bitter, but the truth, however sharp, cut cleaner than deceit. He was the heir to a weapon’s legacy. It did not feel as diminishing as it once might have.

“And now the weapon questions its purpose,” Lira whispered, a ghost of a smile touching her lips. “Good. A blade that cannot think is a tool for tyrants.”

Kael stood, Dawnbreaker’s weight familiar again, but the connection to it felt altered. It was not just a divine gift; it was a lineage, a responsibility. “Marcus hunts it. The Shadow Council believes it will rewrite the world’s wrongs. You and I know it only erases them, and the erasures… bleed.” He remembered the phantom ache of cities un-made, of histories unwritten into the void.

“Then we must reach it before they do,” Lira said, accepting his hand to rise. She was frail, but her resolve was iron. “The Unmade Gate. It lies beneath the heart of Nordmark, where the ley-lines are oldest and most volatile.”

A sudden, deep thrum vibrated through the stone floor, not from the sea, but from *below*. Dust sifted from the ceiling. A section of the wall, depicting Nordmark’s glacial ranges, shimmered.
