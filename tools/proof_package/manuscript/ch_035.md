# Chapter 35: The Burden of the Echo

Kael stared at his hands, half-expecting to see them fade like the glacier around him. Instead, they were solid, scarred, and cold. The absence of Dawnbreaker’s warmth was a physical ache. He stood in the ruins of the glacial chamber, the Obsidian Heart gone, leaving only a fractured pedestal and a silence that rang with its absence.

“You’re still here,” Lira said softly, her hand on his shoulder. Her touch was tentative, as if she feared he might dissolve.

He turned. The scholar’s face was etched with exhaustion and a new, wary awe. “The fragment,” she whispered, pointing to his chest. “It’s… settled. Like a stone in deep water. What did you bargain, Kael?”

He couldn’t meet her eyes. “I saw it. All of it. The timeline Marcus wanted, the Shadow Council’s dominion. A dead world of perfect, silent order. I couldn’t…” His voice cracked. He had chosen to unravel the moment of his own death, to cheat the timeline, but the Heart had demanded a price beyond physical strength. It had taken his clarity, his unwavering belief in the Sunstrider code. It had left him with the echo of his own desperation.

“The Heart is gone,” Kael said, finally meeting her gaze. His eyes, once bright with dawnlight, were now shadowed, holding a depth she hadn’t seen before. “But it left a… a splinter. A piece of its perception. I can feel the threads, Lira. The ones Marcus wanted to pull. They’re frayed here, in Nordmark. The Heart’s removal caused a… a temporal scar.”

As he spoke, a low rumble shook the ice beneath their feet. From the cracks in the chamber walls, a faint, dark mist began to seep—not smoke, but a chilling distortion in the air, smelling of dust and stagnant time.

Lira’s breath caught. “A scar that’s bleeding. The barriers between moments are weakening here. This is what the Heart was meant to contain.”

Kael felt a pull in his chest, the Obsidian fragment humming in resonance with the distortion. It wasn’t a call to power, but a cold, clear map of the damage. He saw the weakness, the point where the timeline was thinnest. And beyond it, in the fading mist, he saw a fleeting image: Marcus, alive, standing in a shadowed hall, speaking with a figure crowned in smoke.
