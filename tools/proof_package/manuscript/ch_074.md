# Chapter 74: The Scholar's Gambit

The temporal storm raged not with wind, but with ghosts. Kael saw Lira’s face ripple, her Elven features momentarily aging a thousand years before snapping back to youth. He saw his own sword, Dawnbreaker, flicker with the dull patina of a century’s neglect. The Sanctum was a crossroads of every moment it had ever contained, and they were drowning in the traffic.

“It’s not just remembering!” Lira’s voice was strained, cut through the chaos. She had one hand braced against a trembling wall, the other gripping a satchel of scrolls. Her eyes, usually so calm, held a scholar’s feverish intensity. “The Heart is *calculating*. It’s projecting temporal fractures outward, propagating them. If we don’t anchor *this* moment, the unraveling will reach Valdris by dusk!”

Kael parried a phantom echo of a falling stalactite that was also a caved-in roof. “How do we anchor what isn’t stable?”

“We don’t. *I* do.” Lira’s hand dove into her satchel. Her fingers closed not on parchment, but on a slender, silvery rod—a temporal stave from the Lyceum’s forbidden vaults, a risk she’d taken long before they’d met. Its surface was etched with equations that shifted under scrutiny. “The stave can focus my will, my memory, into a single, consistent point. I can become the anchor.”

“That’s suicide! The strain—” Kael began, but Lira was already moving.

She stepped into the heart of the chaos, where the floor was neither stone nor memory, but a swirl of potential. Placing the base of the stave on the ground, she didn’t chant; she *remembered*. Her lips moved soundlessly, her eyes glazing over not with magic, but with perfect, unwavering recollection. She anchored herself to the moment they had entered the Sanctum—the smell of cold air, the precise angle of Kael’s torchlight on the archway, the weight of her own doubts.

The vortex of time shuddered. A terrible, high-pitched whine filled the air as the conflicting histories crashed against her focused will. Kael saw blood begin to trickle from her nose. The stave in her hand glowed with a soft, silvery light, not fighting the storm, but weaving it. She was stitching the moment back together, thread by painful thread.

The ghosts solidified, then faded. The shaking stilled. The Sanctum was once again just a chamber of dark stone and a pulsating, malevolent artifact. But the quiet was heavier than the chaos.
