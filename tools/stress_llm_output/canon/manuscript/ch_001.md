# Chapter 1: Ashes Before Dawn

The village of Thornwall had been burning for three days when Kael arrived.

He stood at the ridge's edge, Dawnbreaker sheathed across his back, and watched the smoke curl upward like fingers reaching for a sky that had already turned its face away. The sword hummed against his spine — it always did when death lingered close, as though the blade remembered what it had been forged to end.

Below, bodies lined the road between collapsed homes. No soldiers. No warriors. Just mothers, elders, children. Kael had seen such things before, in the borderlands between Valdris and Eryndor, where the Shadow Council's reach stretched longest. But familiarity did not breed numbness — not yet. He held onto that like a man gripping a ledge.

"They were looking for something," said Lira, stepping from the tree line behind him.

The Elven scholar moved without sound, as her kind always did, though Kael had stopped being startled by it months ago. Her silver hair was pulled back tight, and her dark eyes — ancient despite her youthful face — carried the weight of someone who catalogued horrors in the margins of old texts.

"How do you know?" Kael asked without turning.

Lira held up a fragment of parchment, burned at its edges. "Because they left this behind. A directive from the Shadow Council, written in Marcus's own hand."

Kael took the parchment. The words were half-devoured by flame, but what remained was enough:

*...the Heart lies beneath the Three Stones, sealed by the blood of the last Sunstrider who touched it. Bring me the warrior who carries Dawnbreaker. Bring me Kael.*

His jaw tightened. He read it again, slower, as though the letters might rearrange into something less damning.

"They didn't find what they were looking for," Lira said carefully. "Because you weren't here."

"And these people died for it." Kael folded the parchment and tucked it into his belt. The weight of it felt heavier than steel. "The Obsidian Heart. You've read about it in the archives?"

Lira hesitated — an uncharacteristic pause. "Legends only. Fragments older than the kingdoms themselves. They say it can turn back the river of time, reverse what has been done." She paused again. "But every account ends the same way. The user pays in years. In memory. In self."

"Marcus doesn't care about cost."

"No," Lira agreed. "He doesn't."

Kael descended the ridge toward the village. Dawnbreaker's hum grew louder, a low and mournful note, as if the blade mourned where his voice could not. He knelt beside a fallen woman whose arms still cradled an empty space where a child had been.

He closed her eyes.

When he stood, something had shifted behind his expression — the quiet grief of a man who had carried duty like a torch, only to realize it illuminated what he could not protect.

"The Three Stones," he said. "I know where they are. The elders of my clan spoke of them when I was young — warnings not to go near, though they never said why."
