# Chapter Three: What the Fire Left Behind

The door swung open without a sound.

Elara stood on the threshold, her hand still on the handle, and studied the room beyond. Basement level three was smaller than she'd expected—a single aisle of shelves stretching into the dimness, lit by gas lamps that flickered in the damp air. The red leather volumes Phipps had mentioned were exactly where he'd said they would be, their spines gilt with dates and reference numbers.

But something was wrong.

The dust on the floor told the story. A thin layer of gray covered most of the stone, undisturbed for months or years—but there was a clear path through it, leading directly to aisle seven. Recent footprints, two sets, both wearing the same size and style of boot. Someone had come this way more than once, and recently.

"We're not the first," Elara murmured.

"Those prints." Maren crouched, studying the marks. "Military sole. Standard Reformation issue. See the crosshatch pattern? That's the Council's private security, not the city watch."

Elara felt the familiar chill of being one step behind. The fake courier tonight, the oiled lock, the footprints—someone was very interested in the Ashworth records, and they had resources that extended far beyond a simple document office.

"Stay close. Watch the stairs."

She moved down the aisle, her fingers trailing along the shelf labels until she reached fourteen. The red leather volumes were thick and heavy, their contents documenting the official record of one of the most significant events in the district's history: the fire that had supposedly consumed the Ashworth estate and everyone inside it.

Elara pulled the first volume from the shelf—1847, January through June—and carried it to the reading table at the end of the aisle. The gas lamp overhead sputtered, casting unsteady light across the pages as she opened the cover.

The records were meticulous. Every fire in the district, logged by date, location, cause, and casualties. She flipped through the pages quickly, scanning the entries until she reached October.

There. October 14th.

*Ashworth Estate, 14 October, 1847. Cause: undetermined. Origin: library wing. Casualties: 7 confirmed, 2 presumed. Notable: Fire burned with unusual intensity; water ineffective. Archive materials destroyed. Estate sealed by order of the Reformation Council.*

Elara read the entry twice, then a third time. Water ineffective. She had never heard that detail before—not in the official histories, not in the whispered stories that circulated among the document traders and archivists who made up her professional network.

Fires that resisted water were not natural fires. They were the product of Restricted practices, the kind of thing the Reformation Council had supposedly eradicated decades before 1847.

She turned the page, looking for the follow-up investigation. What she found instead was a gap—a series of pages that had been carefully removed, their stubs still visible along the binding. Someone had excised the record of what happened after the fire.

"Maren. Come look at this."

Her assistant approached, her footsteps quiet on the stone floor. She leaned over Elara's shoulder, studying the damaged volume.

"Pages removed," Maren said. "Professionally. See how clean the cuts are? Someone used a razor, not tearing. This was done deliberately, by someone who knew what they were doing."

"And recently." Elara touched one of the remaining stubs. The paper was still slightly rough at the cut edge, not smoothed by age. "Within the last few months, I'd guess. Maybe weeks."

"The same people who left the footprints."

"The same people who sent the fake courier." Elara sat back, her mind racing. "They're not just watching me, Maren. They're cleaning up. Making sure the official record matches their version of events."

"Which means the original record must have contradicted it somehow." Maren pulled the second volume from the shelf—1847, July through December—and began her own examination. "If we can find out what was removed, we'll know what they're hiding."

Elara nodded, but her attention had been caught by something else. Tucked between the damaged pages, barely visible against the binding, was a thin strip of paper—a bookmark, perhaps, or a note left behind by accident. She eased it free with careful fingers.

The paper was the same quality as the letter that had arrived tonight. The same yellowed edges, the same faint shimmer of Restricted ink. And written on it, in the same precise but trembling hand:

*She was not in the library when it burned. Look to the garden.*

Elara's breath caught. Her mother—Cassandra Voss, née Ashworth—had supposedly died in the library wing, where the fire had started. It was one of the few details everyone agreed on: the Ashworth family had been gathered in the library for some kind of meeting, and the fire had consumed them before they could escape.

But this note claimed otherwise.

"Elara?" Maren had noticed her stillness. "What is it?"

She handed over the note without a word. Maren read it, her expression shifting from curiosity to something harder.

"If this is true—"

"Then the official record is a lie. We already suspected that." Elara pulled the third volume from the shelf—this one containing the supplementary reports and witness statements. "But a lie this big, maintained for seventeen years... that takes resources. That takes power."

"The Reformation Council."

"Or someone behind them." Elara opened the volume, scanning the index. The witness statements should have been recorded here—testimony from the servants who survived, from the neighbors who saw the fire, from the officials who responded. But when she found the relevant pages, they were blank.

Not removed. Blank. As if they had never been written on at all.

"Maren. Look at this."

Her assistant came to stand beside her, and Elara held the page up to the gas lamp. In the right angle of light, faint traces became visible—not ink, but the impression of writing that had been erased. Words that had been committed to paper and then scrubbed away, leaving only ghostly shadows behind.

"Can you read any of it?" Maren asked.

Elara squinted, tilting the page slowly. The impressions were fragmentary, most of the words lost beyond recovery. But a few phrases emerged from the shadows:

*...saw her leave the library before...*

*...garden path toward the old chapel...*

*...flames that burned without fuel...*

*...the child was taken...*

The child. Elara felt the words like a physical blow. In 1847, she would have been twelve years old. The official story said she had been away at school that night—a convenient explanation for the one Ashworth who had survived the fire that ended the line.

But she hadn't been at school. She had been at the estate. She remembered the fire—remembered the heat, the screaming, the way the flames had seemed to reach for her with deliberate intent. And she remembered being carried away by someone whose face she had never seen, someone who had wrapped her in a coat that smelled of smoke and ink and something else, something that made her think of old books and secret rooms.

She had been twelve. And then she had been in a hospital across the city, with no memory of how she'd gotten there, and a man claiming to be her uncle telling her that everyone she loved was dead.

"Elara." Maren's hand was on her arm, her voice urgent. "You've gone pale. What is it?"

"The note said to look to the garden." Elara's voice sounded strange to her own ears, distant and hollow. "There was a garden at the Ashworth estate. My mother's garden. She spent hours there, tending plants that weren't supposed to grow in this climate—flowers that bloomed at night, herbs with no known use. My father used to joke that she cultivated secrets instead of roses."
