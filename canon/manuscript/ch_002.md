# Chapter Two: The Serpent's Teeth

The footsteps stopped.

Elara and Maren stood frozen in the dim office, the fading luminescence of the letter casting strange shadows across the walls. The silence stretched, thick and suffocating, broken only by the distant drip of rainwater from the eaves.

Then came the knock. Three measured raps against the door, pause, then two more. A pattern.

Maren's grip on her crossbow relaxed fractionally. "That's the Archive courier signal."

"At this hour?" Elara moved the letter beneath a leather blotter, leaving the key in her palm. Its iron was warm now, almost hot, as if it had been sitting near a fire. "Since when does the Archive send couriers after dark?"

"Since never." Maren crossed to the door, positioning herself to one side. She glanced back at Elara, one eyebrow raised in silent question.

Elara nodded. She slipped the key into her skirt pocket and moved to stand behind her desk, where the drawer held a second, smaller crossbow—and, more useful, a vial of the same alchemical compound Maren carried. The compound was a simple defensive measure: when exposed to air, it produced a thick, acrid smoke that burned the eyes and lungs. Effective for escape. Less effective for interrogation.

Maren opened the door.

The man on the threshold was tall and thin, dressed in the gray coat of an Archive courier. His cap was pulled low, shadowing his face, and he held a sealed document case in one gloved hand. Rain dripped from his shoulders, forming a small puddle on the floorboards.

"Delivery for Miss Voss," he said. His voice was flat, professional, but there was something off about his posture—a stiffness that didn't match the easy slouch of the regular couriers Elara had dealt with over the years.

"From the Archive?" Maren kept her hand near her belt, her body angled to block any attempt to enter the room.

"From the Office of Cultural Preservation." The man extended the case. "Signatures required."

The Office of Cultural Preservation was a branch of the Reformation Council—the very body the letter had warned her not to trust. Elara's skin prickled.

"I'm not expecting anything from that office," she said from behind her desk. "What's the nature of the delivery?"

"Regulatory matter, Miss Voss. Concerning your license to practice." The courier's hidden eyes shifted toward her, and she felt the weight of his attention like a physical thing. "You are still operating as a document authenticator, are you not?"

The question was too casual. Too careful.

"My license is current," Elara said. "Renewed three months ago. You can verify with the district office."

"This is a supplementary inquiry. New regulations." The case remained extended, waiting. "You'll need to sign for it."

Maren hadn't moved. Her stance had shifted almost imperceptibly, weight dropping into her hips, ready to move in any direction. She caught Elara's eye and gave the smallest shake of her head.

Don't sign. Don't accept. Don't acknowledge.

Elara had trusted Maren's instincts for six years. She wasn't about to stop now.

"I'll need to see your credentials," Elara said. "And I'll need to verify the delivery order with the district office before I sign anything. Regulations work both ways, I'm afraid."

For a long moment, the courier didn't move. The rain continued to drip from his coat, the puddle at his feet spreading slowly. Then, very deliberately, he raised his free hand to his cap and tipped it.

"Of course, Miss Voss. I'll return in the morning with the proper documentation." His voice had changed—still flat, but with an undercurrent of something that might have been amusement. Or threat. "Do have a pleasant evening."

He turned and walked away, his footsteps echoing down the stairwell. Maren closed the door immediately, sliding the bolt into place.

"That wasn't a courier," she said.

"No."

"His gloves. Did you notice?"

Elara had. The gloves were fine leather, too expensive for a courier's wages. And on the left hand, visible for just a moment when he'd tipped his cap, she had seen the edge of a tattoo: a serpent eating its own tail.

The same symbol as the watermark. The same symbol as the key.

"Someone is very eager to know whether I received that letter," Elara said. She pulled the key from her pocket and held it up, studying the serpent worked into its bow. "Eager enough to send someone within hours of its arrival."

"Which means they're watching." Maren moved to the window again, scanning the street below. "Or they knew the letter was coming and wanted to see how you'd react."

"Or they sent the letter themselves, and this was the second part of the trap." Elara turned the key over in her fingers. The metal was still warm, pulsing faintly against her skin like a second heartbeat. "Test the subject, then observe the response."

Maren turned from the window, her expression grim. "What do we do?"

We. The word settled into Elara's chest, heavy and warm. In six years of working together, through forged documents and desperate clients and more than a few close calls, Maren had never once suggested walking away. It was one of the reasons Elara trusted her—perhaps the only person she truly trusted.

"We find out what this key opens," Elara said. "But not tonight. Not while we're being watched."

"The letter mentioned the Thornfield Gate. Midnight on the equinox." Maren's brow furrowed. "That's five days from now."

"Then we have five days to prepare." Elara retrieved the letter from beneath the blotter, smoothing its creased surface. The luminescence had faded entirely now, but she could still feel the warmth where the words had glowed. "And five days to verify what we can."

"Starting with the Ashworth fire."

"Starting with my mother."

The Merrow District's records office occupied the basement of the district hall, a damp, poorly lit space that smelled of mold and old paper. The clerk on duty—a weary man named Phipps who had been working the night shift for longer than anyone could remember—barely looked up when Elara and Maren descended the stairs.

"Miss Voss." He pushed the sign-in ledger across the counter without being asked. "Back again. Research or verification?"

"Research." Elara signed her name with the pen he provided, noting that the ink was the cheap, legal variety—no shimmer, no iridescence, no hint of the Restricted. "I need access to the 1847 records. Specifically, the fire at the Ashworth estate."

Phipps finally looked up, his rheumy eyes sharpening. "That's an odd request. What's your interest?"

"Professional curiosity." Elara kept her tone light. "I'm authenticating a document that references the estate, and I need to confirm the details."

It was a plausible lie. Document authenticators frequently consulted the records office to verify historical details, and the Ashworth fire had been significant enough to appear in any number of legal documents. Phipps nodded slowly, then reached beneath the counter for a ring of keys.

"Basement level three. Aisle seven, shelves fourteen through sixteen. The fire records are bound in red leather. You'll need to sign for each volume you remove."

"Of course."

The stairs to the lower levels were narrow and steep, the stone walls sweating moisture in the damp air. Maren followed close behind, one hand resting on her belt.

"Notice anything?" Elara murmured.

"Phipps didn't ask for my signature. He always asks for my signature."
