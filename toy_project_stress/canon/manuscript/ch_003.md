# Chapter Three: The Old Ways

The wounded traveler slept, but the village of Millhaven did not.

Kael stood at the forge's entrance, watching torchlight move behind shutters up and down the street. Word traveled fast in small places—faster than any imperial messenger could carry it. By now, half the village knew that Maren had brought a stranger through the storm, that his wounds were like nothing the herb-woman had ever seen, that his blood had run black before she could clean it.

The other half would know by morning.

Behind him, the forge had been cleaned with military precision. Maren had helped him stack the kindling, hang the tools, wipe down the anvils—anything to keep her hands busy while they waited for Torren to return from the herb-woman's cottage. Anything to avoid looking at the traveler's sword, which Kael had wrapped in burlap and shoved beneath the workbench.

The blade still whispered. Even through the rough cloth, even over the hiss of cooling coals, he could hear it. Not words—not yet—but the same urgent murmur that had called to him from the scrap steel two days ago. The same voice that had spoken his name.

"You're doing it again."

Kael started. Maren sat cross-legged on the floor near the quenching trough, her back against the stone wall, her gray eyes fixed on him with unsettling directness.

"Doing what?"

"Listening to something no one else can hear." She tilted her head, dark hair falling across her cheek. "Your face changes. You get this look, like you're trying to remember a dream."

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"Liar." She said it without accusation, the way she might observe the weather. "You've been doing it since that morning in the forge. When Torren was working the spring steel. I came to bring breakfast, and you were standing there with your hand out, reaching toward the anvil like—" She stopped, shook her head. "Like it was calling you."

Kael's throat tightened. "You saw that?"

"I see most things." Maren drew her knees up to her chest, wrapping her arms around them. In the dim light, she looked younger than her sixteen years—and older, too. A contradiction Kael had never quite figured out. "I didn't say anything because I thought maybe you'd tell me. Eventually. But then this traveler shows up with his strange wounds and his strange sword, and now you're flinching at sounds nobody else hears, and Torren's been gone for hours doing who-knows-what, and I think maybe it's time you told me what's happening."

"I don't know what's happening."

"That's not the same as not knowing anything." Maren's gaze didn't waver. "Spit it out, Kael. I'm not going to think you're mad. After what I saw tonight—after what I saw in that man's blood—I don't think mad even exists anymore."

The torches guttered in a sudden draft. Rain had given way to wind, and the forge's chimney moaned like a wounded animal. Kael glanced at the burlap-wrapped blade beneath the workbench, then back at Maren.

"You'll think I'm touched in the head."

"Probably. Tell me anyway."

He took a breath. Then another. The words came out rough, like he was pulling them from somewhere deep and reluctant.

"The steel talks to me." He waited for her laughter, her disbelief. When none came, he continued: "Not all steel. Not most of it. But sometimes—when Torren's working, when the metal gets hot enough—I can hear it. Like it's trying to tell me something. Like it has a voice."

Maren was quiet for a long moment. Her expression didn't change.

"And the traveler's sword?"

"Won't shut up." Kael ran a hand through his hair, grimacing at the grit of ash and sweat. "It's been whispering since the moment Maren—since the moment you brought him in. I can barely hear anything else when it gets going. It's like—like someone's standing right behind me, speaking a language I almost understand."

"Almost?"

"Sometimes I catch words. My name, maybe. Or something that sounds like my name." He swallowed. "This morning, when I was cleaning up, I thought I heard it say 'wake.' Or 'awaken.' Something like that."

The wind howled. Somewhere in the village, a door slammed.

Maren unfolded herself from the floor, standing in one fluid motion. "Show me."

"Show you what?"

"The sword. Unwrap it."

Kael hesitated. The burlap bundle seemed to pulse in his peripheral vision, a trick of the light that he knew wasn't a trick at all. "Torren said not to touch it. He said—"

"Torren's not here." Maren crossed to the workbench, her movements careful but unafraid. "And I'm tired of being told to wait while strange things happen around me. If there's something wrong with that blade, I want to see it. If there's something *right* with it, I want to see that too."

She reached for the burlap.

"Don't!"

The word burst from Kael before he could stop it. Maren froze, her fingers inches from the cloth, her eyebrows raised.

"Why?"

Because I don't know what it will do. Because the whispering gets louder when I get closer. Because I'm afraid that if you touch it, you'll hear it too, and then I won't be able to pretend I'm mad anymore.

"Because—" He groped for a reason that wouldn't sound like madness. "Because it might be dangerous. The traveler was wounded defending it. Whatever's in that blade, someone wanted him dead because of it."

Maren considered this. Then she withdrew her hand, stepping back from the workbench.

"All right. I'll wait." Her voice was steady, but Kael could see the tension in her shoulders. "But when Torren gets back, I'm asking him about it. About all of it. The whispers, the wounded man, the reason he went pale as milk when he saw that sword. He knows something. He's known something for years, and he's never told us."

"You think so?"

"I know so." Maren's jaw tightened. "Why do you think he never lets us work the ancient forge? The one in the back room, behind the iron door? He says it's too dangerous, that the techniques were lost, that we're not ready. But I've seen him go in there. Late at night, when he thinks everyone's asleep. I've heard him through the walls, and it doesn't sound like any smithing I've ever heard."

"What does it sound like?"

Maren's gaze drifted toward the back of the forge, where the iron door stood sealed and silent.

"Singing," she said quietly. "It sounds like singing."

The door to the street burst open.

Kael spun, grabbing a hammer from the workbench—then froze as Torren stumbled through the entrance, rain-soaked and breathing hard. The old smith's face was gray beneath its weathered tan, and his eyes were wild with something Kael had never seen there before.

Fear.

"Lock it," Torren gasped. "Bar the door. Now."

Kael didn't argue. He slammed the heavy oak door shut, sliding the iron bolt home just as Torren collapsed against the workbench. The old man's chest heaved; his hands, usually so steady, trembled like leaves in a storm.

"Torren." Maren was at his side in an instant, her hand on his shoulder. "What happened? Is the traveler—"

"The traveler will live. The herb-woman's skilled." Torren coughed, wiped his mouth with the back of his hand. "But his wounds... they're not natural. I've seen their like before. Thirty years ago, in the mountains, when—" He stopped, shook his head. "No. That's not the point. The point is that he was followed."

"Followed?" Kael's grip tightened on the hammer. "By who?"

"Not who. What." Torren pushed himself upright, his gaze sweeping the forge with the practiced assessment of a man checking for threats. It settled on the burlap-wrapped blade beneath the workbench, and something flickered across his face—relief, maybe, or resignation. "There are things in the dark, boy. Things that serve the Emperor's will in ways that aren't written in any chronicle. They've been hunting that traveler for weeks. They nearly caught him in the Thornwood, and they would have, if he hadn't found the river."

"What things?" Maren demanded.

"The kind that leave wounds like his." Torren met her eyes. "The kind that make a man's blood run black."

Silence fell, broken only by the wind's lament and the distant rumble of thunder. Kael thought of the traveler's injuries—the jagged tears in his flesh, the way the edges had seemed to wither rather than bleed. He'd assumed it was infection, some sickness carried on the blade that cut him. But now...

"The sword," Torren continued, his voice dropping low. "Where is it? Did you touch it?"

"No." Kael set the hammer down, suddenly aware of how tightly he'd been gripping it. "I wrapped it in burlap, like you said. I haven't—"

"Not yet." Torren's laugh was harsh, without humor. "But you wanted to. I could see it when I came in. You were standing over it like a man stands over a grave—afraid to approach, but unable to walk away."

Kael couldn't deny it.

"The blade speaks to you." It wasn't a question. Torren pushed away from the workbench, his posture shifting from exhaustion to something harder, more focused. "Don't look so surprised. I've been watching you for weeks, boy. Ever since you first started flinching at the anvil's voice. I was wondering when you'd finally admit it."

"You knew?" Maren rounded on him. "You knew he could hear the steel, and you didn't say anything?"

"What would you have had me say?" Torren's tone softened slightly. "That your friend was touched by something old and strange? That the gift runs in his blood, whether he wants it or not? That the last person I saw with that gift died screaming in a fortress that doesn't exist on any map?" He shook his head. "Some truths are too heavy to drop on a child's shoulders. I was waiting until he was ready."

"And now?"

Torren turned to Kael. The fear was still there in his eyes, but something else had joined it—a desperate, almost pleading hope.

"Now the choice is his."

Kael felt the weight of the moment settle over him like a smith's apron, heavy and inescapable. "What choice?"

Torren crossed to the iron door at the back of the forge. Kael had passed it a thousand times, always assuming it led to nothing more than storage—broken tools, scrap metal, the accumulated debris of a lifetime's work. But now, as the old smith laid his hand against the surface, Kael heard something from beyond it. Not the whisper of steel, but something deeper. A resonance that seemed to vibrate in his very bones.

"This forge was built before Millhaven existed," Torren said quietly. "Before the Emperor's line claimed the throne. Before the kingdoms themselves were more than tribes squabbling over hunting grounds. The people who built it... they knew secrets that the modern world has forgotten. They could make steel do things that shouldn't be possible—hold light, carry memory, speak to those with the gift of hearing."

He pressed his palm flat against the iron. The door groaned, then swung inward on hinges that moved without sound.

Beyond it was darkness—and in the darkness, fire.

Not the orange glow of a conventional forge, but something colder. Bluer. A flame that danced without fuel, without heat, casting shadows that seemed to move of their own accord. Kael's breath caught in his throat as he stared into the ancient chamber, seeing things he couldn't quite comprehend: anvils arranged in patterns that hurt the eye, tools that bore no resemblance to anything in Torren's workshop, and at the center of it all, a stone plinth holding a blade that glowed like captured moonlight.

The whispering surged.

Kael staggered, catching himself on the workbench as a chorus of voices filled his skull—dozens of them, maybe hundreds, all speaking at once in that almost-language he couldn't quite parse. He heard his name again, unmistakable this time. He heard *wake* and *rise* and *forge*. And beneath it all, thrumming like a heartbeat, a single word repeated over and over:

*Choose. Choose. Choose.*

"Make it stop." He pressed his hands to his ears, though he knew it wouldn't help. "Torren, make it—"

"The voices won't stop, Kael." The old smith's hand found his shoulder, grounding him. "They've been waiting too long. But you can learn to hear them differently. You can learn to listen without drowning."

"Why me?" The words came out raw, desperate. "Why can I hear this when no one else can?"

"Because your blood carries the old gift." Torren's grip tightened. "The gift of the Forge-Masters, who shaped destiny itself on anvils like these. Your father had it too."

Kael went still.

"My father was a farmer. He died when I was six. He never—"

"Your father was Aric Vossyn, last of the Forge-Masters' line." Torren's voice cracked on the name. "He hid you here when you were barely old enough to walk. He begged me to keep you safe, to keep you hidden, to never let you near a forge until you were old enough to understand what you were. And I tried, Kael. Gods help me, I tried."

The whispering faded to a murmur, as if the voices themselves were holding their breath.

"But a gift like yours can't be buried forever," Torren continued. "It finds ways to surface. It finds the steel, even when the steel doesn't want to be found. And now—" He gestured toward the chamber, toward the blade that still glowed with its cold, impossible light. "Now the world has found you anyway. That traveler is proof of that. His enemies will be proof of it too, soon enough."

Kael looked at the ancient forge, at the tools and anvils and the blade that called to him with a voice he couldn't ignore. He thought of the broken sword he'd found in the scrap pile, the one whose whisper had started all of this. He thought of the wounded stranger, bleeding black blood in the herb-woman's cottage, hunted by things that left wounds like withered flesh.

He thought of running. Of leaving Millhaven, changing his name again, disappearing into some distant village where the steel stayed silent and the world made sense.

But even as the thought formed, he knew it was useless. The voices would follow. The gift would surface again, and again, until he faced it or went mad.

"Show me," he said quietly. "Show me what the old ones could do."

Torren's breath caught. For a moment, the old smith looked like he might argue—might try to talk Kael out of it, send him to bed, buy more time the way he'd been buying it for sixteen years.

Then his shoulders dropped, and he nodded.

"Step into the forge, then." He moved aside, clearing the path to the open door. "But understand this, Kael: once you enter, you cannot unlearn what you find there. The knowledge of the Forge-Masters is not a cloak you can shrug off when it suits you. It will change you. It will bind you to a war that started before you were born and won't end until you or your enemies are dead."

Kael looked at Maren. She stood by the workbench, her face pale but her eyes fierce, and she gave him the smallest nod.

Whatever happens, I'm here.

He didn't deserve that kind of loyalty. He wasn't sure he deserved any of this—the gift, the knowledge, the chance to be something more than a blacksmith's apprentice with a name he couldn't escape. But the whispering steel didn't care about deserving. It cared about choosing.

And Kael made his choice.

He stepped through the door.

The cold fire parted around him like water, and the voices rose in a chorus that was no longer words but music—ancient, terrible, beautiful. The blade on the plith blazed brighter, and Kael felt something awaken in his blood that had been sleeping his whole life.

He reached for the hammer that hung on the wall.

And the forge sang.
