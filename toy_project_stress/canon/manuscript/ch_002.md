# Chapter Two: The Wounded Traveler

The rain came sudden and hard, the way it always did in the weeks before the harvest—sky clear one moment, then bruised black and weeping the next. Kael stood in the forge's doorway, watching sheets of water turn the road into a river of mud. The morning's strange encounter with the whispering steel had settled into something easier to ignore. A trick of exhaustion. Too many hours breathing coal smoke and too few hours sleeping.

That's what he told himself.

Behind him, Torren worked the bellows with steady patience, feeding air to banked coals that had no blade to shape. The old smith hadn't spoken much since morning. His silence had a different quality than usual—heavier, like he was listening for something he didn't want to hear.

"Should close the shutters," Torren said finally. "Storm'll damp the coal."

Kael nodded but didn't move. Something about the rain held him. The way it blurred the world beyond the forge, softening edges, making familiar things strange. The village of Millhaven lay just visible through the downpour—thatch roofs and stone walls, the market square's wooden stalls hunched like old men against the weather. Home. It had been home for six years now, ever since Torren found him shivering outside this very doorway, a child of eight with no memory of where he'd come from and nothing to his name but a broken sword fragment too dull to cut butter.

The broken blade. He still kept it, wrapped in oilcloth beneath his sleeping mat. He didn't know why. Couldn't have explained the compulsion if someone asked—and no one ever had, because no one knew it existed. Sometimes, late at night, he unwrapped it and traced the jagged edge with his fingertip, feeling the strange warmth that lived in the metal. The same warmth he'd felt this morning, when the finished blade had whispered to him.

*Stop it.* He shook his head, scattering the thought like the rain scattered light. There was work to do. Shutters to close. Coal to protect. A life of small tasks that kept the world simple.

Then he saw the figure on the road.

At first, just a shape in the rain—staggering, slow. Kael squinted, thinking it might be old Brennan from the mill, deep in his cups again. But the shape resolved as it drew closer, and it wasn't Brennan. It was a woman, tall and lean, supporting another figure half-dragged across the mud. The second person hung limp, head lolling, feet leaving furrows in the muck.

"Torren." Kael's voice came out strange, tight. "There's someone—"

"I see it." The old smith was already beside him, peering into the storm. His expression had changed. The distant, listening look was gone, replaced by something sharper. Warier. "Get inside, Kael."

"But they're hurt—"

"Inside. Now."

Kael had heard that tone exactly three times in six years. Once when a trader's horse went mad in the market square. Once when the river flooded and threatened the lower village. And once when a stranger wearing the Emperor's crest had passed through Millhaven, asking questions about orphaned children.

He'd never forgotten how Torren's hand had felt on his shoulder that day—tight enough to bruise, pushing him toward the back of the forge, toward the hidden cellar beneath the coal store. *Stay there. Don't come out until I tell you.*

But this wasn't the Emperor's men. This was a woman struggling with a wounded traveler, and they were still fifty paces from the forge, and the rain was coming down harder, and Kael couldn't just watch.

He stepped out into the storm.

"Kael!" Torren's voice cracked like a whip behind him, but Kael was already running, mud sucking at his boots, rain driving against his face. The woman saw him coming and something flickered across her features—relief, maybe, or suspicion. Hard to tell through the downpour.

"Help me," she called, and her voice was steady despite the weight she bore. "He's bleeding out."

Kael reached them and finally saw the wounded man clearly. Traveler's clothes, road-worn and torn. A face pale as winter ash, eyes closed, lips slightly parted. And blood—so much blood, soaking through a makeshift bandage wrapped around his torso. The cloth had once been white. Now it was dark and wet, and the rain wasn't washing it clean so much as spreading it, painting red streaks down his side.

"Take his arm," the woman said. "Over your shoulder. I've got his other side."

Kael obeyed, sliding beneath the man's arm. Up close, he caught a smell beneath the rain and blood—something sharp and metallic, but not iron. Not the smell of the forge. Something else. Something that made the hair on his arms stand up, though he couldn't have said why.

They half-carried, half-dragged the wounded man toward the forge. Torren stood in the doorway, and his expression was a war—duty against caution, compassion against something that looked almost like fear. But when they reached the threshold, he stepped aside.

"Bring him in. Lay him by the hearth." The words came out clipped, reluctant. "Maren." He nodded at the woman. "Didn't expect to see you back this way."

"Didn't expect to be back." Maren shifted her grip, helping Kael lower the wounded man onto the stone floor near the forge's warmth. Up close, Kael could see her more clearly—sharp features, dark hair cut short and practical, eyes the color of river slate. She was young, maybe twenty, but there was something worn in her face that made her seem older. A scar traced a thin line from her left temple to her jaw, pale against sun-darkened skin. "Found him on the East Road, about two miles out. Someone cut him bad and left him for dead."

"Robbers?" Torren was already moving, gathering clean cloth from the shelf by the quenching trough.

"Maybe." Maren's tone said she didn't believe it. "Robbers take the coin purse. They don't leave the boots, the cloak pin, the—" She stopped herself, glancing at Kael. "The other things."

What other things? Kael wondered, but he was already kneeling beside the wounded man, pulling back the soaked bandage to assess the wound. The gash ran from just below his ribs to his hip, deep enough to show the red gleam of muscle beneath. It needed stitching, and soon, or the man wouldn't last the hour.

"I'll need needle and thread," Kael said. "And the salve Torren makes—the one with woundwort and yarrow."

"Smart boy." Maren's voice held a note of surprise, or maybe reassessment. "You a healer?"

"Smith's apprentice." Kael pressed fresh cloth against the wound, feeling the man's blood hot against his palm. "But I've sewn up cuts before. Burns, too. You learn, working the forge."

Torren returned with the supplies, setting them beside Kael with careful hands. The old smith's face was unreadable, but his eyes kept moving—between the wounded man, between Maren, between the door. Watching.

"Kael." Torren's voice was low. "When you've done what you can, go to the back. Check the coal stores."

The coal stores. Where the hidden cellar was. The message was clear: *Be ready to hide.*

"I'll stay until he's stable," Kael said, and it wasn't defiance exactly—just a statement of fact. The man was bleeding, and Kael was the one who could help, and he wasn't going to run and cower in a dark hole while someone died on the forge floor.

Torren held his gaze for a long moment. Then he nodded, once, and moved to stand by the door, arms crossed, watching the rain.

Kael threaded the needle with hands that didn't tremble. He'd done this before—small cuts, burns, once a deep gash on Torren's forearm when a piece of slag had gotten under the smith's guard. But never anything this bad. Never with this much blood, this much at stake.

He bent to his work.

The first stitch drew a groan from the wounded man, and his eyes flew open. Kael froze, caught by that sudden gaze. The man's eyes were dark, almost black, and filled with a pain that went deeper than the wound in his side. His hand shot out, gripping Kael's wrist with surprising strength.

"The blade," the man whispered. His voice was barely audible above the rain and the forge's crackle. "They'll come for the blade. You have to—"

"Save your strength." Kael tried to pull free, but the grip was iron. "You're hurt. Let me—"

"Listen." The man's eyes were wild, desperate. "The Whispering Steel. It's not a legend. It's real, and they know, and they'll burn this whole village to find it. You have to hide it. You have to—"

His grip loosened. His eyes rolled back, and he went limp against the stone floor.

Kael sat back on his heels, heart hammering. The Whispering Steel. The same words Torren had spoken this morning, when the finished blade had sung its strange song. *Some call it the Whispering Steel. Metal that remembers. Metal that speaks to those who know how to listen.*

He looked at Torren. The old smith stood frozen in the doorway, and his face had gone the color of old ash.

"You knew," Kael said. It wasn't an accusation. Not yet. Just a statement, a piece of a puzzle he was only beginning to see. "You knew this would happen. That's why you didn't want me to go out there. That's why you're telling me to hide."

"Kael—"

"The whispering I heard this morning. The blade. It wasn't exhaustion, was it? It was real." Kael's hands were shaking now, the needle still threaded, blood drying on his fingers. "What's happening, Torren? Who is this man? And who are they—the ones he says will come?"

Torren opened his mouth, closed it again. The war on his face was visible—decades of silence battling against something new, something urgent. His eyes went to Maren, who stood by the hearth with her arms wrapped around herself, watching.

"You know something about this," Kael said to her. "That's why you brought him here, isn't it? Not to the village healer. To the forge."

Maren's jaw tightened. "I used to live in Millhaven. Before I left, six years ago. Torren was—" She hesitated, glancing at the old smith. "He was more than a blacksmith, then. And I thought, if anyone could help, if anyone would know what to do with a man raving about enchanted blades..."

"Enchanted." Kael tasted the word. It should have felt strange, impossible. But it didn't. It felt like the warmth in the broken sword fragment. Like the whisper of the finished blade. Like something he'd always known but never had words for. "The steel is magic."

"The steel is *old*," Torren said finally. His voice was heavy, tired, like a man setting down a burden he'd carried for too long. "Older than the Empire. Older than the kingdoms that came before. And yes, it has... properties. Qualities that the uninitiated might call magic." He moved away from the door, coming to stand over the unconscious man. "I don't know who this traveler is. I don't know who hurt him. But if he knows about the Whispering Steel, if he's talking about it on the roads where anyone can hear..."

"Then the wrong people might already know," Maren finished. "I passed riders on the road yesterday. Three of them, wearing dark cloaks, not talking to anyone. I thought they were just merchants, but merchants stop at inns. These ones didn't. They just rode, like they had somewhere specific to be."

Torren's face went even grayer. "Which direction were they heading?"

"West. Toward Millhaven."

The silence that fell was thick enough to cut. Outside, the rain began to ease, and in the quiet, Kael could hear the banked coals of the forge breathing, could almost hear them whispering. Or maybe that was his imagination. Maybe that was always his imagination.

*Was it?*

He looked down at the unconscious man, at the wound he'd only half-stitched, at the blood still seeping through the cloth. This stranger had come with a warning, and the warning was already coming true. Riders on the road. People looking for the Whispering Steel. And somewhere in this forge—or hidden in the cellar beneath it—was a blade that could sing.

Kael made his choice.

He turned back to the wounded man, bent over the half-finished work, and drove the needle through skin and flesh with steady hands. One stitch. Two. Three. Each one a small defiance against the fear that wanted him to run, to hide, to pretend he'd never heard the steel whisper.

"Tell me everything," he said, not looking up from his work. "About the Whispering Steel. About what you were, before you were a blacksmith. About who might be coming." He pulled the thread tight, tying off a knot. "I'm not going to hide in the cellar, Torren. Not this time."

The old smith was quiet for a long moment. Then, slowly, he moved to the shelf where he kept his finest tools—the ones he never let Kael touch, the ones that seemed too delicate for a village forge. He reached behind them, into a gap Kael had never noticed before, and drew out something wrapped in faded cloth.

"I was going to wait," Torren said. "Wait until you were older. Wait until I was sure." He unwrapped the cloth, revealing a blade unlike anything Kael had ever seen. It was short—more a dagger than a sword—and the metal was dark, almost black, with veins of silver running through it like rivers seen from a mountain's height. The edge caught the forge-light and seemed to hold it, glowing faintly even when the light shifted away. "But it seems waiting is a luxury we no longer have."

The blade whispered.

Not aloud—not exactly. But Kael felt it, felt the vibration in his bones, felt the warmth spread from his chest to his fingertips to the base of his skull. The whisper wasn't words. It was something older than words. Something that spoke directly to the blood.

*Finally. You're ready to hear.*

He didn't know if the voice was real or if he was losing his mind. But he knew, with a certainty that went deeper than reason, that his life had just changed forever.

"What is it?" His voice came out as a croak.

"It's a piece of what was broken," Torren said. "A fragment of the last blade forged in the old way, before the Empire came and the Smiths were scattered. Your father gave it to me, Kael. Gave it to me the night he died, and told me to keep it safe until you were old enough to claim it."

*My father.* The words hit like a hammer blow. In six years, Torren had never spoken of Kael's parents. Never explained where he'd come from, why he shared a surname with the Emperor, why he'd been left alone in the world with nothing but a broken sword and no memory of his life before.

"My father," Kael repeated. "You knew him. You knew who I was this whole time, and you never—"

"There wasn't a choice." Torren's voice was rough. "There was only keeping you alive. And that meant keeping you ignorant, because what you don't know can't be tortured out of you." He held out the blade, hilt first. "But now they're coming, and ignorance won't protect you anymore. Nothing will protect you, except what you're willing to learn."

Kael stared at the blade. The silver veins seemed to pulse, just barely, like the metal was breathing. The whisper was still there, at the edge of hearing, patient and waiting.

Outside, the rain stopped entirely. And in the sudden silence, somewhere in the distance, Kael heard hoofbeats on the road.

Maren heard it too. Her head snapped toward the door, and her hand went to her belt—to the knife Kael hadn't noticed she was wearing, tucked beneath her cloak.

"How far?" she asked Torren.

"An hour, maybe less. If they're who I think they are." Torren's eyes were ancient now, full of years and grief and things Kael couldn't begin to understand. "We don't have much time."

Kael reached out and took the blade.

The moment his fingers closed around the hilt, the whisper became a roar—a flood of sensation and sound and something that might have been memory, though not his own. He saw a forge lit by strange fire. He felt hands that weren't his hands shaping metal that wasn't ordinary metal. He heard voices chanting in a language he didn't know but somehow understood, each word a command, each syllable a promise.

Then it faded, and he was back in the forge, the blade steady in his grip, Torren and Maren watching him with expressions that mixed fear and hope in equal measure.

"I heard it," Kael said. "I heard the steel. It showed me—" He shook his head. "I don't know what it showed me. But it was real. It's all real."

"Yes," Torren said. "It is. And now you have a choice, Kael. The same choice your father made, the same choice every Smith's child has made since the Empire rose. You can run. Hide. Hope they don't find you. Or you can stay, and learn, and fight."

The hoofbeats were closer now. Three riders, Maren had said. Three riders in dark cloaks, heading west toward Millhaven.

Kael looked at the blade in his hand. He thought of the broken sword fragment beneath his sleeping mat—the piece of himself he'd never understood. He thought of the wounded man on the floor, raving about burning villages. He thought of Torren, who'd kept him alive for six years, who'd lied to him for six years, who was looking at him now with something that might have been pride or might have been sorrow.

"I'm not running," Kael said.

He turned back to the wounded traveler and bent to finish his work. The needle went in, the thread pulled tight. Each stitch a promise. Each knot a declaration.

Behind him, Torren began to gather tools—hammer and tongs and the strange implements Kael had never been allowed to touch. Maren moved to the door, knife drawn, watching the road.

And in Kael's hand, the dark blade whispered its approval.

The riders were coming. But for the first time in his life, Kael Vossyn was ready to meet them.
